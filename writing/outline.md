# Key Data Points & Outline

## Global domestic retention rates (% of squad playing at home)

| Confederation | 1994 | 1998 | 2002 | 2006 | 2010 | 2014 | 2018 | 2022 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| UEFA | 64% | 58% | 50% | 57% | 51% | 48% | 33% | 34% |
| CONMEBOL | 62% | 42% | 45% | 32% | 19% | 17% | 15% | 11% |
| CONCACAF | 80% | 77% | 73% | 59% | 46% | 48% | 28% | 50% |
| AFC | 96% | 91% | 63% | 65% | 49% | 41% | 45% | 58% |
| **CAF** | **36%** | **33%** | **22%** | **20%** | **20%** | **10%** | **15%** | **12%** |

---

## Points of note

### 1. The global shift is real and steep
- Every confederation has seen domestic retention fall since 1994
- UEFA leagues are now the effective development system for the entire world game
- CONMEBOL shows the sharpest non-CAF decline: 62% → 11% over 30 years

### 2. CAF started lowest and fell furthest
- Already the lowest domestic rate in 1994 (36%), well below everyone else
- Collapsed to ~10% by 2014; has since stabilized at 12–15%
- By 2022, ~75% of CAF World Cup players are in UEFA-member leagues

### 3. Senegal 2022: the extreme case
- 0 of 26 players at a domestic Senegalese club
- 26 of 26 playing in UEFA leagues
- Reached the Round of 16

### 4. Ghana 2022: nearly as stark
- 23 of 26 players based in Europe
- Only 2 playing domestically

### 5. Morocco 2022: diaspora as semifinalists
- Just 3 of 26 players from the domestic Botola Pro
- Reached the World Cup semifinal — the best-ever result for an African nation
- Underscores that domestic retention ≠ competitive quality

### 6. Nigeria: the long-run constant
- Zero domestic players at 1994 and 1998 World Cups
- Has essentially never fielded a squad that reflects domestic Nigerian football
- The Super Eagles are consistently an elite-European-league team in disguise

### 7. Tunisia: the exception that faded
- 1998: 18 of 23 players domestic — highest CAF rate in the dataset, by far
- 2006: 4 of 23; 2018: 6 of 23; 2022: 8 of 26
- Still the highest CAF domestic rate in 2022, but a shadow of the 1998 anomaly

### 8. South Africa 2010: the host effect
- As hosts, 16 of 23 players were from domestic clubs — anomalously high for CAF
- Did not advance from the group stage; retention ≠ quality

### 9. AFC is the counter-trend
- ~58% domestic in 2022, *up* from a low of 41% in 2014
- Saudi Arabia and Qatar field nearly all-domestic squads (state investment + league wealth)
- Gulf leagues are now wealthy enough to retain — and attract — competitive talent

### 10. The Gulf corridor: a new pipeline for African players
- 2018: 10 African World Cup players in AFC (Gulf) leagues, mostly Egyptian (Al Ahly, Zamalek domestic + Al-Taawoun, Al-Ittihad, Al Fateh for Saudi clubs)
- 2022: 12 African World Cup players in Gulf clubs across 4 nations:
  - Cameroon: Aboubakar (Al-Nassr), Epassy (Abha), Fai (Al-Tai)
  - Tunisia: 5 players in Qatar and Kuwait
  - Morocco: Hamdallah (Al-Ittihad), Mohamedi (Al-Wehda), Benoun (Qatar SC)
  - Ghana: André Ayew (Al-Sadd)
- Africa's export relationship with wealthy leagues is no longer exclusively European

## Scrollytelling states

### State 1 — 1994 overall
**Job: establish the baseline**
The world as it was. AFC nearly fully domestic (96%), CONCACAF at 80%, CONMEBOL at 62%. CAF is already the lowest at 36%, but the gap isn't yet alarming — it reads as one outlier among broadly similar confederations.

### State 2 — Nigeria 1994
**Job: introduce the sharpest edge of the story early**
0 of 22 players in the domestic Nigerian league. Every other squad at that tournament has majority-domestic players. Nigeria is a preview of where the whole continent is heading. A bar that is entirely Europe is visually striking when almost everything else is mostly domestic.

### State 3 — Tunisia 1998
**Job: the anomaly / subverted expectation**
18 of 22 players domestic — the highest domestic retention any African side ever recorded. It briefly suggests that the Nigeria pattern isn't inevitable. The payoff comes later when Tunisia's domestic rate collapses to 4/23 by 2006 and never recovers. This state plants a question the later states answer.

### State 4 — South Africa 2010
**Job: the host effect and its limits**
As hosts, South Africa brings 16 of 23 domestic players — the only time a CAF side has had majority-domestic retention in the modern era. Nigeria is at the same tournament with 0/23. Two African squads, same year, at opposite poles. Neither advances from the group stage, puncturing any assumption that retention correlates with quality.

### State 5 — 2014 overall (Algeria / Cameroon as examples)
**Job: the nadir — the outflow at its most complete**
CAF drops below 10% domestic for the first time. Algeria, Ghana, and Cameroon each bring 1–2 domestic players total out of 23. Good moment to zoom out and show the full confederation comparison — CAF is now unambiguously in its own category, and the export of African talent to European leagues is essentially total.

### State 6 — Morocco 2022
**Job: the payoff / open question**
3 of 26 players from domestic Moroccan football. The rest play in Europe or the Gulf. And yet: semifinalists, the best result in African World Cup history. The state that asks what "domestic football" even means as a measure of identity — when your best team is, by residency, mostly French and Spanish.
